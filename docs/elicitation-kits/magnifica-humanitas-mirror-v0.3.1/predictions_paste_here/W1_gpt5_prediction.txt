There, human beings possess one language and remarkable technical capacity; yet their project, closed in upon itself, becomes a monument to self-sufficiency and ends in dispersion. The second is Pentecost (cf. Acts 2:1-11). There, difference is not abolished, but reconciled by the Spirit; language becomes communion, and power is placed at the service of witness. These images remind us that the question is not whether humanity can build, compute or organize at an ever greater scale, but whether its works remain open to God and to the cry of the poor. As the Second Vatican Council taught, “earthly progress must be carefully distinguished from the growth of Christ’s kingdom,”8 yet it can contribute to a more human ordering of society when charity governs knowledge and power, and justice protects the vulnerable, especially children.
