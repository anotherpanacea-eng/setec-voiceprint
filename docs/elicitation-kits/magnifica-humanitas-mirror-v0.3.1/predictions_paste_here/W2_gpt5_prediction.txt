We are thus called to discernment. The Lord does not ask us to abandon the works of human intelligence, but to place them within the covenant of love. Every algorithm, every network and every machine must be measured by the face of the person whom Christ has redeemed (cf. Jn 3:16), especially the poor, the vulnerable and those without voice. When technical progress forgets this face, it becomes what Saint John Paul II called a “structure of sin” (cf. Sollicitudo Rei Socialis, 36); when it serves integral human development, it can become an instrument of peace and fraternity (cf. Populorum Progressio, 14; Fratelli Tutti, 8). For this reason, the Church does not stand before the digital age as a fearful spectator, but as a mother and teacher who asks: does this progress enlarge communion, protect conscience and safeguard creation, or does it merely accelerate consumption and control?
