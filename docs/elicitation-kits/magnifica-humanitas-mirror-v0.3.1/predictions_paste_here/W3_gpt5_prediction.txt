Precisely because AI can serve many noble purposes, we must not receive it with fear alone, nor with naive enthusiasm. Like every work of human ingenuity, it is called to be placed at the service of the common good and morally judged according to the dignity of the person, “the image of God” (Gen 1:27), and the demands of justice. The Church has often recalled that technique is never neutral when it enters the fabric of social life; it expresses choices, priorities and visions of humanity (cf. Gaudium et Spes, 15; Laudato Si’, 102). Therefore, we ask that these instruments be developed and used with transparency, accountability and effective human oversight. Where decisions touch life, freedom, work, education, health care or the administration of justice, no person may be reduced to a profile, a prediction or a score, for “the sabbath was made for man” (Mk 2:27), and never conversely.
