They are not merely literary figures but signs that disclose two opposed orientations of the human heart, and therefore two possible directions for the journey of civilization. In the Book of Genesis, the descendants of Noah, gathered in the plain of Shinar, resolve to build a city and a tower whose top reaches the heavens, "to make a name for ourselves." Their project is not condemned for its technical daring, nor for the ambition to build in common. What the sacred text places in question is the underlying intention: a humanity that, refusing the limits of its creaturely condition, seeks self-sufficiency apart from God and pursues unity through the imposition of a single language rather than through the patient communion of distinct voices freely offered to one another and God.
