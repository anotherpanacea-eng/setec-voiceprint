There, humanity, gathered in a single place and speaking a single language, sets out to build a city and a tower whose top reaches the heavens, "to make a name for ourselves." The project is not condemned for its technical ambition but for its underlying intention: a humanity that, refusing the limits of its creaturely condition, seeks self-sufficient mastery and a unity imposed by power rather than received as a gift from God. The result is confusion of tongues and dispersion across the whole earth. The narrative warns us that when human capacity is severed from the recognition of God and from the bonds of fraternity, it produces not communion but fragmentation, not understanding but the multiplication of mutual incomprehensions among peoples.
