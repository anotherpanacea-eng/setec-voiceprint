The descendants of Noah, gathered in the land of Shinar, propose to build a city and a tower whose summit reaches the heavens, in order to "make a name for ourselves" and avoid being scattered upon the face of the earth. What the sacred text presents is not a condemnation of human ingenuity as such, nor of the legitimate desire to build and to organize common life. Rather, it discloses the ambiguity that arises when technical capacity becomes detached from a right ordering of ends: when the work of human hands seeks to substitute itself for the gift of communion, and when unity is pursued through the imposition of uniformity rather than received through patient fraternal dialogue.
