10. The temptation of Babel, we must recognize, is not confined to a distant antiquity. It returns in every age, assuming new forms suited to the means available. In our own day, it appears in the dream of a total mastery over nature, over history and over the human person itself, pursued by means of technologies that promise to abolish every limit and every dependence. This "Babel syndrome," as we may call it, manifests itself wherever progress is conceived as a unilateral ascent of the human will, unaccompanied by gratitude, by wonder before creation, or by the recognition that we are not the authors of our own being. It manifests itself when efficiency is exalted as the supreme criterion, when speed replaces discernment, when scale supplants encounter and when the language of algorithms is permitted to crowd out the patient grammar of human conversation, and the slow ripening of wisdom.
