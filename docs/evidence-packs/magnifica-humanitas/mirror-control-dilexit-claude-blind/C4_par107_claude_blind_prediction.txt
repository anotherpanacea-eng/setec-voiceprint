For Bonaventure, the wound in Christ's side is not merely a historical fact to be remembered but a living door through which the soul may enter and abide. He writes with tender insistence: "Having found this heart, both his and mine, I shall pray to my God." The heart of the Lord becomes the dwelling place of the believer, and the believer's heart, in turn, becomes the dwelling place of the Lord. 107. This reciprocity, this mutual indwelling, is at the very core of Christian spirituality. It is not a private mysticism reserved for an elite, but the ordinary vocation of every baptized person. To enter the heart of Christ is to learn to love as he loves, to suffer with those who suffer, to rejoice with those who rejoice, and to offer oneself, without reserve, for the salvation of the world. The medieval mystics understood this with remarkable clarity.
