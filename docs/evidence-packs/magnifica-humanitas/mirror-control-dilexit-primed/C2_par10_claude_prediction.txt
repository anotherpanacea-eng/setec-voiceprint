10. The consequences of this loss are visible all around us. When the heart is silenced, the human person is reduced either to a calculating machine or to a bundle of urges to be managed. Both reductions, though seemingly opposed, share a common refusal: they refuse to acknowledge that we are made for communion, for love, for a wisdom that exceeds what can be measured or consumed. Saint John Paul II warned, in this regard, against an anthropology that severs the spiritual from the bodily, leaving the person “fragmented” and incapable of recognizing his or her own dignity. Benedict XVI likewise reminded us that “being Christian is not the result of an ethical choice or a lofty idea, but the encounter with an event, a Person”. Such an encounter cannot take place on the surface of life. It requires a depth that only the heart, attentive and unhurried, can provide.
