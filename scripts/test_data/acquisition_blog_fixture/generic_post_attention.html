Conditions of Attention

Attention has conditions, in the sense that one cannot simply decide to pay it. The decision matters; the conditions matter more. A reasonably constant set of physical surroundings, a sufficient and not excessive amount of sleep, a state of not-too-recent and not-too-distant emotional weather — without these, the decision to pay attention runs aground on the shore of a self that is not, at this moment, paying any.

One can fight against the conditions, of course. I have done this often enough, and the fighting produces work, but the work is more brittle than the work I produce when the conditions are merely permissive. Brittle work is recognizable: the sentences are correct but they do not breathe; the structure is intact but the joints are tight; the reader passes through the paragraph without pausing because the paragraph does not afford a pause. Permissive-conditions work breathes. The sentences turn over. There are openings the reader can step into and find something inside them.

I cannot reliably produce permissive conditions. What I can do is recognize when they are present and not waste them on tasks that do not need them. The administrative writing — the e-mails, the framing of decisions that need to be made anyway — can be done in any weather. The actual writing should wait, when it can, for the weather that suits it. This is not always possible. It is more often possible than I initially believe.

The discipline, then, is double: to recognize when the conditions are present, and to set the work aside when they are not. The first half is easier than it sounds, with practice. The second half is harder than it sounds, in any season.
