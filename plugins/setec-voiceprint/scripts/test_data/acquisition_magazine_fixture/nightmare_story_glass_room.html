The Glass Room

By Synthetic Author A
March 15, 2019

The room had been there since before the house was hers; she had only inherited it, the way one inherits a habit, and like most habits it had taken her years to notice. The walls were glass on three sides and the fourth was a door that opened onto a garden she had never properly tended. From the door, the room looked unfinished — bare floorboards, a single chair, no curtain — and from the garden it looked like a proposition she did not yet have the language to refuse.

She had grown up in houses that announced their rooms by their furniture: the dining room held the dining table, the study held the books, and the rules of association ran in one direction only. The glass room, by these rules, should not have been a room at all. It held nothing. It contained the chair the way a sentence contains a verb — necessary, unobtrusive, easily overlooked. Whatever the room was for happened around the chair, and you only learned what it was for by sitting in the chair long enough to find out.

What she found out, after some weeks of sitting, was that the glass room collected light differently from the other rooms. The garden bled in through the walls in a way the kitchen window did not allow. The light arrived without filter and without permission. She had spent her life in rooms whose walls obstructed weather; here the weather sat down beside her and waited to see what she would do with it.

What she did at first was nothing. Then she began bringing books. Then she began bringing nothing again, because the books made the light less interesting, and the light was the point. By autumn she had stopped bringing herself, in any deliberate sense; she went to the room when the light asked her to. The asking, by then, was clearly distinguishable from her own decisions. She did not know how to explain this, even to herself, and so she did not.

The garden, meanwhile, did what gardens do when they are not tended: it produced, with patient indifference, exactly the plants the soil could afford. The glass room watched this without commentary. She watched it through the room. The two of them, room and garden, had been in agreement for longer than she had been their custodian, and her job — she came to understand — was not to interrupt.
