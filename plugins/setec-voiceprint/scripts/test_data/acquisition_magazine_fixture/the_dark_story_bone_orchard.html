The Bone Orchard

By Synthetic Author C
June 1, 2019

The orchard had been planted by someone who no longer remembered planting it. This was not unusual in our county. Acreage of that period had been worked over so many times by so many tenants that the question of who had set down which tree was a question without an answerable shape, and people who tried to ask it tended to be told, gently, that they were asking the wrong thing.

What was unusual about the orchard was its grid. The trees had been planted in a pattern that did not correspond to any of the planting manuals my grandfather kept on the porch. They were spaced too closely for fruit trees and too widely for hedge trees, and the rows ran north-northwest, which is the direction the wind pushed in a season we did not have. Whoever had planted the orchard had done so for some purpose that did not survive their planting it.

The neighbors said the orchard was full of bones. They said this casually, in the way that small-town people say things they have never personally verified but consider settled. The bones had nothing to do with anything; they were a feature of the orchard, like the unfruitful trees or the unaccountable grid. If you wanted to verify the bones, you went to the orchard, and either you found bones or you didn't, but the saying was independent of the finding. The orchard was full of bones because the saying made it so.

I went to verify, the year I came home. The trees were as crooked as I remembered. The grid held in the way grids hold, with the indifference of something that was once intentional and is now only persistent. I walked the rows for an afternoon and found nothing. I walked them the following morning and found a small bone, which I did not pick up. I left it where it was, and I left the orchard, and I went home, where the saying had already made room for what I had found.

That was the year I learned that some places are made by the things that are said about them, and that going to verify them is one of the ways you keep saying. I did not return to the orchard. I have continued to say.
