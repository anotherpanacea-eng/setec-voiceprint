Quiet House

By Synthetic Author B
March 22, 2019

The house had been quiet for so long it was beginning to seem suspicious. Quiet houses are not, by themselves, unusual; the suspicious quality came from the consistency of the quiet. A normal house produces a normal floor of small sounds — radiator clicks, joists settling at temperature, the faint friction of someone elsewhere being home. This one produced none of those. It produced the silence one expects from a room one is not in, and produced it equally regardless of which room one was in.

The previous tenant had left a note. It said only: "There is nothing wrong with the house." The note was written in pencil, in a hand that suggested the writer had taken some care to seem casual. She had read it twice, the second time looking for what was being denied, and then she had pinned it to the inside of the kitchen cabinet she used for tea. She did not show it to her husband. He noticed it three weeks later, on his own, and asked her about it without seeming to find it strange.

The first oddity she noticed was the way her own footsteps did not echo. Footsteps in a house with hardwood floors and minimal furniture should produce a small reverberation; hers produced nothing. The carpet in the bedroom did not produce nothing; it produced the muffled sound carpets produce. The hardwood produced nothing. After some weeks she started walking with deliberate force, the way one walks when checking that a porch is sound, and the floor still did not respond.

Her husband, who was a calm and observant man, did not seem to notice the silence. He spoke at his usual volume; he listened to records at his usual volume; he laughed at things that struck him as funny, and his laugh filled the kitchen the way it had filled their previous kitchens. The silence, whatever it was, was not stealing his sound. Nor was it stealing hers, in any way she could pin down. Her voice was her voice. The records were the records. What was missing was what should have surrounded them — the room's contribution to the noise it was supposed to be hosting.

She did not, at any point, conclude that something was wrong with the house. The previous tenant had been clear about that. What she concluded, more slowly, was that something was wrong with the relationship the house had to its sounds. This was a different finding, and a less actionable one. Houses cannot be advised about their relationships to sound. They can only be lived in, until the relationship reveals what it is going to demand.
