The Quiet Room

The room I write best in is one I built deliberately and then half-forgot. I painted the walls a color I liked, put a window where the light came in from a direction I liked, set down a chair I had picked out for years before it was finally available, and arranged the books and papers in a way that suited the kind of work I expected to do. Then I sat down to do the work, and the room receded.

The receding was, I now think, the point. A workroom that demands attention from the writer is a workroom doing its job badly. The eye should slide off it. The hand should know where the pen is without having to look. The window should let the sky do whatever it is doing without imposing the news of weather on the page. A workroom is, ideally, the kind of object that earns its keep by being mostly invisible — which is also true, I suspect, of the best prose, but I do not want to push the analogy too hard.

What the room gives me, on the days when it is doing its job, is a particular quality of silence. Not literal silence; the city is outside, and I have never lived where one could be free of distant traffic and intermittent voices. The silence I mean is internal. It is the absence of the small auditory weather of one's own attention — the to-do list rehearsed under the breath, the snippet of last night's conversation replayed for the eleventh time, the sentence one is about to write rephrased preemptively into something safer than it should be.

That kind of silence is rare, and it cannot be summoned by act of will. It comes when it comes. What the room can do, what any deliberately arranged workspace can do, is reduce the friction enough that when the silence arrives there are no obstacles between it and the work. The chair is already comfortable. The pen is already at hand. The page is already where the page goes. Nothing is fighting for the attention that is being offered. The work begins.
