Older Essay From the Archive

By Test Author · November 30, 2017

What we keep and what we discard becomes, at some scale of accumulation, a portrait of our judgment. I have been thinking about this in connection with my own archive, which is now large enough to have an internal weather: storms in some sections, long stretches of overcast in others, and a few unaccountable bright afternoons when whatever I was reading or seeing or hearing seemed to fall together in ways I did not earn.

The bright afternoons are rare, and they tend to leave the lightest marks. The drafts I produced during them are not, in retrospect, my best drafts; the sentences are loose, the connections too easy, the work of compression unfinished. I once thought this was a failure of execution. I have come around to thinking it is a feature of the underlying state. Bright afternoons feel like something has been given to you, and the writing they produce wants to be a thank-you note rather than a piece of work. Thank-you notes have their place, but they are not, I think, what most readers come to a writer's archive for.

So I distrust the bright afternoons now, in the practical sense that I do not trust drafts produced in them as finished. I let them sit. I come back when the storm has resumed. The storm is uncomfortable but it is honest; it forces me to argue with my own first instinct rather than simply transcribing it; the prose that survives that argument has more weight than the prose that didn't have to.

This is, I admit, an unfashionable position. It runs against most contemporary advice about writing, which tells the writer to follow the energy and trust the flow. I follow the energy too, but I have learned not to commit to it until the energy has had a chance to fail.
