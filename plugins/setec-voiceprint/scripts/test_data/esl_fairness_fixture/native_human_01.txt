My grandfather kept bees, and the summer I turned eleven he handed me a veil that smelled of woodsmoke and old canvas. He never explained much; he expected the hives to do the explaining. So I watched. I learned that a calm hand carries its own weather, that bees read hesitation the way a dog reads fear, and that the worst thing you can do near a hive is flinch and apologize for it afterward.

The frames came out heavy and gold. He'd tilt one toward the light, squint, and grunt something that might have been approval or might have been a warning about the weather. I never could tell. That was the point, I think. He wanted me uncertain, leaning in, paying the kind of attention that only uncertainty buys.

By August my arms were stippled with stings I'd stopped counting. The pain went dull and then comic — a tax on curiosity, he called it. We pulled honey in the kitchen with the windows open and the radio losing a baseball game in the next room. He let me run the uncapping knife. I botched it, dripped wax everywhere, swore once, and he laughed so hard he had to sit down.

He's gone now, and the hives went to a neighbor who keeps them better than I ever could. But some mornings I catch that woodsmoke-canvas smell out of nowhere, and I'm eleven again, flinching, learning not to.
