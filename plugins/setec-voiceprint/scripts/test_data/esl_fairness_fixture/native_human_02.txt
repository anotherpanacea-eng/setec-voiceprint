The garage smelled of two-stroke oil and the particular rust that only old tools acquire, the kind that flakes off orange on your palms. My father rebuilt carburetors out there on Saturdays, parts laid out on a towel in an order only he understood, and he talked to the engine more than he talked to me. Not coldly. It was just that the engine asked better questions.

I handed him wrenches I guessed at and was wrong about half the time. He never corrected me sharply; he'd just hold out his hand again, palm up, patient as a tide, until I tried a different size. That was the whole curriculum: try the wrong thing, notice, try the next. By the end of a winter I could name every socket by feel in the dark.

What I remember most is the moment the engine caught. There's a half-second where nothing has happened yet and then everything has, and the whole garage seems to inhale. He'd lean back, wipe his hands on the rag he kept in his back pocket, and say "there she is" like greeting someone late to dinner.

He died the spring I turned thirty, and I sold the truck because I couldn't bear to hear it run without him in the cab. I kept the rag. It's stupid, a greasy square of cloth, and I keep it in a drawer with things that matter, and I cannot explain to anyone why.
