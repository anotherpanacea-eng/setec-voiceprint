I had no sense of being moved. I felt motion in the air. There was nothing but land: not a country at all, but the material out of which countries are made. No, there was nothing but land — slightly undulating, I knew, because often our wheels ground against the brake as we went down into a hollow and lurched up again on the other side. I had the feeling that the world was left behind, that we had got over the edge of it, and were outside man's jurisdiction.

I had never before looked up at the sky when there was not a familiar mountain ridge against it. But this was the complete dome of heaven, all there was of it. I did not believe that my dead father and mother were watching me from up there; they would still be looking for me at the sheep-fold down by the creek, or along the white road that led to the mountain pastures. The wagon jolted on, carrying me I knew not whither. I don't think I was homesick. If we never arrived anywhere, it did not matter. Between that earth and that sky I felt erased, blotted out. I did not say my prayers that night: here, I felt, what would be would be.

Cautiously I slipped from under the buffalo hide, got up on my knees and peered over the side of the wagon. There seemed to be nothing to see; no fences, no creeks or trees, no hills or fields.

The morning of my first day on the farm I awoke before anybody else was stirring. I dressed quickly, and ran downstairs. There was no one in the kitchen, but a fire was roaring in the stove. A kettle was singing. There were dried-apple pies, in three layers, on the table. Through the windows I could see the new sun, low over the bluffs, and the great red barn standing out against it.

Grandmother came in carrying an apron full of corncobs to fire up the stove. She gave me a hug and asked me if I was hungry. She would have breakfast ready as soon as her men came in from the corral. While she was greasing her pans for the corn-bread, she asked, "You wouldn't be afraid to take the milk-pail down to the creek, would you? There's a nice clean spring there."

When I came out of the cellar with the cream, I heard Otto and Jake clattering at the wash-basin by the kitchen door. The kitchen was full of the smell of frying bacon and the sound of eggs sputtering in the pan. The bacon was crisp and curled, the eggs were fried in butter, and the coffee, when grandmother poured it, was black and strong. Jake and Otto came in red-faced and wet-haired and dropped into their chairs and began to eat without speaking. They were hungry men, and the morning was already half gone for them.

— Willa Cather, "My Ántonia" (1918, public domain)
