Chairman and members of the Subcommittee, I appreciate the chance to testify on the governance of interstate data flows. My testimony advances a single claim: that the choice before this body is not between regulation and deregulation, but between a coherent federal floor and a fragmentation that serves no one well.

Begin with the nature of the problem. Data does not respect the jurisdictional lines that much of our existing law presupposes. A rule adopted in one state routinely governs conduct whose effects are felt in another, and the firms subject to those rules operate, of necessity, across many states at once. Any framework that pretends otherwise will produce obligations that conflict, overlap, or simply cannot be satisfied simultaneously.

Against this backdrop, two familiar positions are each incomplete. The first holds that a single national standard is the only workable answer. There is force to this: uniform rules reduce uncertainty and forestall a competition among states to weaken protections. But uniformity overrides local judgments that may reflect genuine differences in values, and it concentrates in one decision a great deal that might better be learned gradually.

The second position holds that states should remain free to set their own terms, treating regulatory diversity as a laboratory. I am sympathetic to the experimental impulse, but the laboratory metaphor fails precisely because the experiments are not contained. When one state's rule imposes costs on the residents of another, the premise that each jurisdiction bears the consequences of its own choices no longer holds.

What I recommend is a cooperative structure: a clearly defined federal floor that addresses the most acute cross-border harms, paired with room for states to exceed it where local conditions warrant. This is not a tidy compromise that splits the difference; it is a considered judgment that the floor resolves the spillover problem the diversity view cannot, while leaving the harder questions of preemption to be settled as evidence accumulates. I urge the Subcommittee to begin there, and I welcome your questions.
