Prepared Statement of Jane Smith, Director, Office of Widgets

    Chairman, Ranking Member, and members of the Committee, thank you for
the opportunity to testify on the oversight of federal widget programs. I
will make three points: that the current oversight framework is misaligned
with how widgets are now manufactured, that this misalignment falls hardest
on small producers, and that a targeted modernization would address the
problem without abandoning the national standard.

    My first point concerns the framework itself. The governing statute was
written before the modern widget supply chain existed. Its technical
definitions assume a manufacturing process that few producers still use, and
as a result compliance is measured against a baseline that no longer
describes the industry. The consequence is not merely administrative
inconvenience; it is that the data the agency collects do not reliably
reflect the risks the program was created to manage.

    My second point concerns who bears the cost. Larger manufacturers
maintain dedicated compliance staff and can absorb the burden of reporting
against an outdated standard. Smaller producers cannot. The evidence my
office has gathered shows that compliance costs, measured as a share of
revenue, fall several times more heavily on small firms. A framework intended
to protect the public has become, in practice, a barrier to entry that
advantages incumbents.

    My third point concerns the remedy. I do not recommend abandoning federal
oversight; a national standard prevents the fragmentation that a patchwork of
state rules would create. I recommend instead that Congress update the
technical definitions to match current practice, align the reporting
requirements with how widgets are actually produced, and introduce a tiered
compliance schedule scaled to the size of the firm. Each of these steps is
incremental, and together they preserve the national market while relieving
the disproportionate burden on small producers. I look forward to your
questions.
