Interstate Data Flows and Regulatory Tradeoffs

The movement of data across state lines has become a central concern for policymakers, and the governance questions it raises do not resolve neatly into existing legal categories. This report analyzes how competing regulatory approaches would shape the flow of information among states, assesses the evidence on each, and frames the choices Congress faces. The argument begins from the observation that data does not respect the jurisdictional boundaries that much of current law presupposes.

One school of thought favors a single national standard. Its supporters argue that uniform rules reduce uncertainty for firms and prevent a race to the bottom in which states compete by weakening protections. The empirical record offers some support: industries that operate under a unified federal standard report lower compliance costs per transaction than those subject to overlapping state regimes. Yet uniformity has a cost of its own, because a national rule necessarily overrides local preferences that may reflect legitimate differences in values across communities.

A contrasting view holds that states should remain free to set their own requirements. On this account, regulatory diversity functions as a laboratory, allowing policymakers to observe which approaches work before committing the entire country to one of them. The objection is that data flows are not easily confined: a rule adopted in one state can impose costs on residents of another, and the resulting spillovers undercut the premise that each state bears the consequences of its own choices.

Between these poles lies a cooperative-federalism approach, under which the federal government sets a floor while permitting states to exceed it. This structure preserves a national baseline against the race-to-the-bottom concern while leaving room for the local experimentation that the diversity argument prizes. The principal difficulty is administrative: firms operating in many states must still track the more stringent requirements wherever they apply, which reintroduces some of the fragmentation the floor was meant to limit.

The report concludes that the choice cannot be made on efficiency grounds alone, because it turns on a prior judgment about how much weight to give local self-determination relative to national coherence. It recommends that Congress, if it acts, begin with a clearly defined federal floor, since that step addresses the most acute spillover problems while leaving the harder questions about preemption to be resolved as evidence accumulates.
