# Layer C: Source Triage

The voice-attribution layer. Answers the question that distributional analysis and pattern-matching cannot: whose voice is this, and is it doing real work?

## The Principle

Almost all the AI-prose work depends on being able to ask "whose voice is this, and is that voice doing real work or pretending to?" Pattern-matching surface tells without that triage produces the failure mode common in external critiques: generic recommendations that flatten character voice in the name of cleaning up prose.

Source triage is the highest-resolution layer because every rhetorical pattern Layer B catches can be earned in one place and unearned in another. The same "Not X. Not Y." formulation is earned when a character is doing structured-thinking voice during a focused activity, and unearned when the narrator does it as commentary three pages earlier. The pattern is identical at the surface; the question is whether voice is doing work, or signaling that work should be happening.

This layer requires voice-bearing material that the writer can identify. For fiction: each character's voice register, plus the narrator's. For nonfiction: the writer's persona, the audience's expectations, the argument's stakes. Without that input, source triage is unreliable. Run Layers A and B and report Layer C as unavailable.

## The Payoff Test

The most actionable diagnostic in the framework. AI-fluent prose often takes the shape:

```
AI-fluent setup → character-voice payoff
```

The setup hedges, qualifies, or aphorizes. The payoff commits, specifies, lands. The setup is doing nothing the payoff isn't doing better. Cut the setup, promote the payoff.

**Example.**

```
His cock stirred. Not fully. Not yet. A pulse of interest, impossible
and faintly comic and profoundly unfair.
```

becomes

```
His cock stirred. A pulse of interest, impossible and faintly comic
and profoundly unfair.
```

The "Not fully. Not yet." was carrying nothing the next sentence didn't already carry better. The negation was hedging toward a commitment that the third sentence already made.

**The diagnostic question.** Does the next sentence do the work the prior sentence pretended to? If yes, cut the prior. The payoff sentence usually has the specific image, the committed verb, the registered emotion. The setup sentence usually has a hedge, a negation, an indefinite, or an aphorism.

**When the payoff test fails.** Sometimes the setup is doing real work: building rhythm, establishing register, doing the cognitive labor of a character actively sorting. Then both stay. The diagnostic is contextual; pattern alone does not entitle the cut.

## The Five Named Patterns (Fiction)

Each of these can be earned or unearned. Source triage decides.

### 1. Negation Hedge

**Forms.** "Not X." / "Not X, exactly." / "Not X. Not Y."

**What it does (unearned).** Narrator pretends to make a careful discrimination. The next sentence does the work the negation pretended to.

**What it does (earned).** Character is actively sorting, cataloguing, or refusing the wrong word. The negation IS the cognitive act.

**Earned example.**

```
She listened. Not for footsteps; he wouldn't come. Not for the dog
either; the dog had stopped barking hours ago. For the click of the
furnace, the only thing that ever told her she was alone in the
house in the way she had to be alone.
```

The character is doing structured listening. Each negation discriminates a real possibility she's tracking. The list of what-it-isn't builds the cognitive shape of waiting.

**Unearned example.**

```
The room was quiet. Not silent, exactly. Not peaceful either. Just
empty in the way old rooms get when no one's been in them for a while.
```

Three negation moves to land on "empty in the way old rooms get when no one's been in them for a while," which says everything the prior two sentences gestured at. Narrator-pose negation. Cut the first two sentences.

**Cut rule.** Cut when the next sentence does the work the negation pretended to. Keep when the character's cognition is the discrimination.

### 2. Indefinite-Pronoun Gesture (also AIC-2 subtype)

**Forms.** "Something" + abstract qualifier. "Some X part" + adjective. "A kind of Y" without specifying Y.

**What it does (unearned).** Outsources specificity to the reader's imagination. The prose gestures at sensation without committing to one.

**What it does (earned, rare).** Character cannot name what they are feeling, and the prose registers that incapacity as part of the experience.

**Earned example.**

```
Something was wrong with her hand. Not pain, not numbness, not pins
and needles. Something else, something that had no name in the
languages she knew.
```

The character is the one searching for the word and failing. The indefinite is diagnostic of her experience.

**Unearned example.**

```
She felt something soft and uncertain rise in her chest, some kind
of feeling she couldn't quite place.
```

The prose, not the character, is failing to commit. "Soft and uncertain," "some kind of feeling," "couldn't quite place" all serve the same evasion. Replace with a concrete image: name the body part, the action, the registered specific.

**Cut rule.** Replace indefinites with concrete imagery wherever possible. Keep only when the character's specific incapacity to name is the point, and even then preferably name the failure ("she had no word for it" rather than "something").

### 3. Disguised Correctio

**Forms.** "Not X, but Y" embedded in narration. "Did not X but Y." Mid-sentence reversal that pretends the narrator made a discrimination but actually just postponed the commitment.

**What it does (unearned).** Same as Negation Hedge but harder to spot because it lives inside a sentence. Almost always cuts cleanly.

**Earned example (rare).**

```
She didn't love him, but she was used to the shape his absence left.
```

The contrast does work: the contrast IS the point of the sentence. Both halves carry weight.

**Unearned example.**

```
He didn't walk into the room so much as drift, his footsteps barely
audible on the wood.
```

"Didn't walk into the room so much as" is doing nothing "drift" doesn't already do. The reversal is decorative. Cut to: "He drifted into the room, his footsteps barely audible on the wood."

**Cut rule.** Almost always cut the negation. Replace with the affirmative form. If the contrast does real work, keep.

### 4. Pseudo-Aphorism

**Forms.** "X as Y." "X is the Y of Z." "There is a kind of X in every Y."

**What it does (unearned).** Aspires to maxim register without earning the standing of a maxim. Often has a real image right after that does the actual work.

**Earned example.**

```
Grief is the rent you pay for love.
```

Lands as a maxim. Carries weight on its own. Does not require an image to follow because the aphorism IS the image.

**Unearned example.**

```
Memory is the architecture of the self. She walked through the kitchen
and the smell of coffee brought her grandmother back, the kitchen in
1987, the radio always on.
```

"Memory is the architecture of the self" gestures at maxim register. The next sentence does all the work. Cut the aphorism.

**Cut rule.** Cut the aphorism, keep the image. Aphorisms earn their place when they carry weight alone, not when they preface the work.

### 5. Manifesto Cadence

**Forms.** Three or four parallel sentences building to conclusion. Anaphora, syntactic parallelism, escalating clauses.

**What it does (unearned).** Parallel structure substitutes for actual development. Each sentence does the same work in a different register.

**What it does (earned).** Each sentence escalates the previous. The parallelism IS the development.

**Earned example.**

```
She had not raised her voice. She had not raised her hand. She had
not, in the end, even raised her eyes when he left.
```

Each sentence pulls back further. The parallelism dramatizes increasing restraint. The structure is the meaning.

**Unearned example.**

```
We must commit to youth. We must invest in our communities. We must
build a better future for those who will come after us.
```

Three sentences saying the same thing in three near-synonymous frames. Manifesto cadence as substitute for argument. Cut to one specific commitment, or rebuild with each sentence escalating.

**Cut rule.** Earned when each sentence escalates, restricts, or reveals. Unearned when each sentence is a register variant of the same content.

## Triage Rules by Passage Type (Fiction)

The same pattern is earned in one passage type and unearned in another. The triage is contextual.

| Passage type | Pattern usually earned | Pattern usually unearned |
|---|---|---|
| Dialogue | Yes; character voice tolerates more pattern | Only when the pattern is uniform across all characters |
| Character interior, actively sorting | Yes; the negation IS the cognitive act | When the sorting goes nowhere |
| Character interior, generic introspection | Rarely | Almost always; "Not X. Not Y." setup is AI-fluent monologue |
| Narrator-pose commentary | Rarely | Almost always; the "There was X in his Y" structures are pose |
| Genre-required language | Yes (hypnotic induction, prayer, ritual) | When the writer is signaling the register without earning it |

The hardest rule to teach is that "earned" depends on context, and only knowing what the character's voice actually is reveals which instances qualify. Without voice attribution, the pattern alone does not justify the cut.

## The Voice Test

For each flagged sentence, ask: is this something this specific character (or this story's narrator) would actually say? Or is this generic literary-fluent interior monologue?

**Generic monologue wearing a character's name is the failure mode.** Real character voice has specific registers. A character with a bureaucratic vocabulary and self-aware comic register sounds different from a character with an occupational, structured-thinking register. Both are "literary"; neither is a generic literary voice.

**Diagnostic.** Take a flagged sentence. Could the same sentence appear in another character's POV without changing? If yes, voice is not doing the work. The sentence is generic.

### Voice slip vs. lost callback

When the voice test flags a line as off, the failure mode matters because the fix is different.

**Voice slip.** The line is in nobody's voice. It's authorial generic register imported into a character's mouth. The character wouldn't say this; the line could appear in another character's POV without changing. Fix: rewrite in the character's voice, or cut.

**Lost callback.** The line is in voice (the character would say this) but reaches for material the reader hasn't seen. The setup was cut in earlier revision and the callback now lands on no anchor. The reader registers the line as discordant because it's gesturing at a shared frame the prose hasn't established. Fix: restore the setup somewhere earlier, or cut the callback, or replace it with a callback to material the current text supports.

The diagnostic question that distinguishes them: when you ask the writer "does the character actually use this phrase elsewhere, or refer to this idea elsewhere?", the answer for voice slip is "no, this is just authorial register." The answer for lost callback is "yes, but you cannot see it because it has been cut." Lost callback is recoverable through restoration. Voice slip requires rewriting.

This distinction came out of testing the framework on a manuscript where a callback ("Still think this is just new horny?") had lost its setup riff in earlier revision. The line tested as off-register against the character's other dialogue in the chapter, but the writer recognized the line as a stranded callback to material that had been cut. The fix was restoration in an earlier chapter, not rewriting at the callback site.

When running source triage and a flagged line resists the voice-slip rewrite (the writer says "no, the character would say this; something else is wrong"), check for lost callback before continuing. Search the rest of the manuscript for the term, the analogy, or the conceptual setup the line is reaching for. Absence is a strong signal that the setup got cut.

## The Five Patterns, Parallel Set (Argument-Shaped Nonfiction)

For testimony, briefs, op-eds, scholarly articles, and policy memos, the equivalent patterns aren't negation hedges and pseudo-aphorisms. They're the rhetorical poses that AI-assisted argument writing falls into.

### 1. Abstraction Shielding

**Forms.** "Stakeholders," "youth-serving systems," "those impacted," "communities of color," "service providers," "decision-makers."

**What it does (unearned).** Lets the writer avoid naming specific actors, specific institutions, specific people. The abstraction protects the writer from commitment and from the reader's ability to verify.

**What it does (earned).** Names a class because the class itself is the analytical unit. "Youth-serving systems" earned when the writer is making a structural critique of how multiple agencies treat young people similarly. Unearned when "youth-serving systems" replaces "DYRS, OVSJG, and DCPS" because the writer isn't sure which is responsible.

**Earned example.**

```
The pattern recurs across youth-serving systems: assessments that
classify rather than diagnose, plans that schedule rather than treat.
```

"Youth-serving systems" is doing analytical work. The pattern is what binds the systems. Replacing with three specific agency names would obscure the structural point.

**Unearned example.**

```
Stakeholders agree that more must be done to support youth in the
community.
```

"Stakeholders," "more," "support," "youth," "community" are all gestures. The sentence commits to nothing. Cut entirely or rewrite with names: "Council members, advocates, and the families we represent agree that DYRS must end placement of youth at PRTFs."

**Cut rule.** Replace with specific actors when the analysis depends on knowing who. Keep when the abstraction is the point of the analysis.

### 2. False-Balance Construction

**Forms.** "While reasonable people may disagree." "There are valid concerns on both sides." "Some have argued [reasonable position]; others have argued [unreasonable position]."

**What it does (unearned).** Smuggles in the appearance of judiciousness while granting standing to positions that don't merit it. Flattens moral or evidentiary asymmetry.

**What it does (earned).** Genuinely contested empirical or normative question; both positions are entitled to the standing.

**Earned example.**

```
Reasonable people disagree about whether the right age threshold for
youth justice jurisdiction is 18, 21, or 25; the developmental
evidence supports the higher thresholds, but reasonable jurisdictional
arguments cut both ways.
```

The disagreement is real. Each position has standing. The writer's commitment (the developmental evidence) is named. The hedge does not flatten asymmetry.

**Unearned example.**

```
While reasonable people may disagree, ankle monitoring has both
benefits and costs that should be carefully weighed.
```

Manufactures balance where evidentiary asymmetry exists. Names no actual benefits, no actual costs, no actual disagreement. Cut to: "Ankle monitoring has produced no demonstrable safety benefit and has caused documented harm."

**Cut rule.** Replace with specific named disagreement when the disagreement is real. Cut when the construction is fabricating balance.

### 3. Hedge-and-Affirm

**Forms.** "While X is generally true, in some cases Y." "Although X, it is also true that Y." "X, though of course Y."

**What it does (unearned).** Performs caution while saying nothing definite. The hedge protects the writer from commitment; the affirm performs commitment without earning it.

**What it does (earned).** Genuine epistemic care. Acknowledges a real qualification before committing.

**Earned example.**

```
While most youth in DYRS commitment will not be re-arrested in the
two years post-disposition, the conditions under which they were
committed predict the conditions of their release more strongly
than program participation does.
```

The hedge is true and load-bearing. The affirm names a specific finding. The structure dramatizes the analytical move.

**Unearned example.**

```
While GPS monitoring may have certain benefits in some cases, it is
also important to consider the potential negative impacts on youth
and families.
```

Both halves are gestures. "Certain benefits in some cases" specifies nothing. "Potential negative impacts" specifies nothing. Cut to specific evidence.

**Cut rule.** Hedge-and-affirm is earned when both halves do specific work. Unearned when both halves gesture.

### 4. Recommendation Template

**Forms.** "DC must commit to..." "We urge the Council to..." "It is essential that the agency..." "Going forward, [stakeholder] should prioritize..."

**What it does (unearned).** Provides the appearance of advocacy without specifying the action. The verb is committed but the object is generic.

**What it does (earned).** Names a specific action by a specific actor by a specific date.

**Earned example.**

```
The Council should reject DYRS's FY27 budget request for expanded
GPS monitoring capacity and redirect those funds to community-based
alternatives that the agency has under-funded for the past three
fiscal years.
```

Specific actor (Council). Specific action (reject the budget request, redirect funds). Specific scope (FY27, GPS expansion, community-based alternatives). Verifiable.

**Unearned example.**

```
DC must commit to investing in evidence-based interventions that
support positive youth development and community safety.
```

Five gestures in one sentence. No actor identified beyond "DC." No action specified. No timeline. No verification possible. Cut entirely or rebuild with specifics.

**Cut rule.** Recommendations earn their place when actor, action, scope, and verifiability are present. Generic recommendations are template-driven and almost always unearned.

### 5. Authority Laundering

**Forms.** "Scholars have argued..." "Research suggests..." "Studies have shown..." "Experts agree..."

**What it does (unearned).** Borrows authority without taking on responsibility. The writer reports what authority claims without committing to it or accountably citing it.

**What it does (earned).** Names the scholar, the study, the finding, with citation. Commits to the authority's claim, contests it, or specifies its scope.

**Earned example.**

```
Scott and Steinberg's developmental-stage analysis (2008) demonstrated
that adolescent risk-taking reflects ongoing neurological maturation
rather than character deficit; subsequent fMRI work (Casey et al.
2011) has localized the relevant prefrontal-limbic dynamics.
```

Names authors, dates, claims. The reader can verify. The writer commits to the framing.

**Unearned example.**

```
Research has consistently shown that young people benefit from
community-based alternatives, and experts agree that punitive
approaches are counterproductive.
```

"Research," "consistently," "young people," "community-based alternatives," "experts," "punitive approaches" are all gestures. The sentence borrows authority without earning it. Cut to specific citations or commitments.

**Cut rule.** Authority claims earn their place when they name the authority and its specific claim. Generic appeals to research or expertise are usually authority laundering.

## Triage Rules by Passage Type (Nonfiction)

| Passage type | Pattern usually earned | Pattern usually unearned |
|---|---|---|
| Argument under construction (working through a problem) | Yes; thinking is the point | When the working-through goes nowhere |
| Recommendation paragraphs | Rarely | Almost always; "DC must..." templates are pose |
| Concession passages | When the concession is real | When false-balance constructions manufacture concession |
| Evidence deployment | When evidence is named and committed to | When authority laundering substitutes for citation |
| Framing / context-setting | When the frame does specific analytical work | When abstractions ("youth-serving systems") replace named actors that the writer should commit to |

## Voice Differentiation Strategy

When Layer B identifies AIC-1 (Generic Hand) at Pattern or Systemic severity, and source triage confirms that voice is not doing the work, the restoration move is voice differentiation across characters or across registers.

For fiction:

- **Each character's self-correction syntax should sound like them.** If Sarah corrects herself by trying alternative formulations ("when you see a thing that needs to be done... when the work appears"), that's structured-thinking voice. David should not correct himself the same way.
- **Each character's uncertainty word should sound like them.** "Something" is unmarked; everyone uses it. But one character can have "a kind of," another "this thing where," another the deliberate refusal to name. Vary at the function-word level, not just the content level.
- **Each character's internal rhythm should sound different.** Sentence length, syntactic complexity, fragment-tolerance, and use of parenthetical asides should vary across POV characters.

For nonfiction with multiple registers (academic, policy, advocacy, blog):

- **Each register has its own pose vocabulary.** Advocacy testimony tolerates "Council members," "the youth we represent," and direct address. The academic register tolerates "the literature," "the empirical record," and qualified claims. The blog register tolerates "look," "here's the thing," and direct sentences.
- **Switching registers is fine; importing the wrong pose vocabulary is the failure.** Academic hedging in advocacy testimony reads as evasion. Direct testimony-style sharpness in an academic article reads as polemic. The diagnostic is whether the register matches the venue and audience.

## "Don't Fake Voice You Can't Sustain"

When cutting AI-fluent prose leaves a vacuum, sometimes a character-specific phrase fills the space better than nothing. But only when the writer has real voice to draw from. Faking voice creates a different kind of pose, sometimes worse than the AI-fluent version.

**Operational test.** Take the proposed character-voice fill. Could the writer reproduce a similar phrase in another scene with the same character, drawing on the same voice register, without searching? If no, the writer faked it. Leave the cut empty.

**Better than faking.** Most cuts heal on their own. The surrounding prose carries the meaning. A reader reading the revised passage almost never notices an absence; they notice presence (a forced character-voice fill that doesn't sound like the character) much more readily.

**When to fill.** When the surrounding prose has voice, and the cut leaves a beat that the voice should occupy. When the writer has multiple sentences in the same character's voice they can draw from. When the fill is obviously consistent with prior characterization.

## Diminishing Returns

First pass through a chapter cuts substantial AI-fluency. Twentieth pass is polishing what's already polished. Stop when the next cut starts replacing voice rather than removing pose.

**Practical heuristic.** After the cuts you're confident about, mark the next ten cuts as "considered." Sit with them for an hour or a day. Re-examine. The ones that still want cutting are real. The ones that don't are the boundary where pose ended and voice began.

**Sign that diminishing returns has arrived.** The cuts you're considering remove material that, if removed, would change the rhythm or register in a way you can't immediately replace. That's the boundary.

## Process Notes

**External feedback triage.** AI critique often produces output proportional to what's asked, including manufactured critique on strong work. Convergence across multiple models or readers (same flag fires from independent sources) is usually signal. Divergence is where the writer's judgment decides. The cleanest test: does the feedback name something verifiable against the actual chapter, or does it pattern-match a generic recommendation?

**Recycle, don't delete.** Cuts go to a recycle file, not to the trash. Material that doesn't fit the tightened story may fit a future story with the same characters. Preserving cuts is preserving optionality.

**Calibration warnings.** Don't cut every instance of a flagged pattern; triage is contextual. Don't replace direct prose with ornate prose; the fix is concrete. The hardest rule to teach is that "earned" depends on context, and only knowing what the character's voice actually is reveals which instances qualify.

## The Single Most Useful Principle

Source triage. If you only carry one move from this layer, carry the question: whose voice is this, and is it doing real work?

Pattern-matching surface tells without that triage produces the failure mode common in external critiques: generic recommendations that flatten character voice in the name of cleaning up prose. Source triage prevents that failure by making voice attribution the precondition for every cut.
